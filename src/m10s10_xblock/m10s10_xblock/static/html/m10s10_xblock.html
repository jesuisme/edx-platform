Map your stakeholders according to level of interest in your project and level of power to impact your project to determine KEY STAKEHOLDERS


  1. The subjects are those who have high interest but low power. They can help to secure understanding and buy-in
  2. The players are those who have both high interest and high power. They will be your main collaborators
  3. The crowd are stakeholders with both low interest and low power, but who should be kept informed of implementation or changes
  4. The context setters have high power but not high interest. These are people who should be consulted and given a voice in proceedings as they influence success

SPC Rules
  • Rule frameworks have been developed over time to help analyze SPC charts
  • These help the user to identify whether a condition is:
    • in control (normal and expected), or
    • out of control (special cause; a non-random occurrence that should be addressed)
  • For instance, the below chart shows four of several available frameworks (select to expand). If you were using the Nelson-Juran framework and your SPC chart showed 9 points in a row above the centerline, it would indicate special cause variation that requires further investigation.

Great job working through this SPC chart activity! We hope you will use these concepts in your improvement projects in the future. Here are three key take away points from SPC charts:

  1. Any process that is measured will have normal and expected variation in this measure over time.
  2. SPC charts allow you to tell the difference between normal, or common cause, variation and special cause variation—unexpected variation that signals the process may have changed
  3. When working on a quality improvement project and using SPC charts, special cause variation tells you when you should investigate a process (something may have gone wrong) or if process improvement has really occurred (your intervention made a difference!)
